# Enclosure Management Guide (JWE3)

## Comfort System
Dinosaur comfort is the core welfare metric and the foundation of a functioning enclosure. Low comfort causes fence attacks, escapes, agitation fights, and poor breeding outcomes. Comfort is divided into two categories:

**Environmental Needs (Paleo Needs)** — terrain type, terrain proportions, food, water, foliage
**Social Needs** — population count, social species count, male/female ratio, cohabitation species

To view a dinosaur's needs: scan it with a Ranger Team or click the dinosaur and open its needs tab. Red bars indicate unmet needs. Needs only have to be in the white — just above the threshold — to count as satisfied; there is no benefit to dramatically exceeding most needs.

A dinosaur's needs are shaped by its Territory. Needs can only be satisfied by conditions within that Territory, not the enclosure as a whole. As population grows, Territories expand and Environmental Needs increase accordingly.

## Territory
When released, a dinosaur wanders until it reaches full comfort, at which point its Territory is fixed. Dinosaurs of the same species sharing space form a shared Territory. Where Territories overlap between species, cohabitation effects apply.

Territory expands naturally as population grows. Juveniles maturing into adults will increase space requirements — plan enclosures with future growth in mind, especially when running a breeding programme.

## Environmental Setup

### Terrain
Each species requires specific terrain proportions. Use the Terrain Paint tool in the Environment menu to apply grassland, sand, rock, wetland, and other surface types. If a species is struggling to meet a terrain threshold, expanding the enclosure area generally helps as it dilutes the unwanted surface ratio.

### Water
All species require water. Land dinosaurs need a pond or lake of appropriate size. Marine reptiles require large lagoon enclosures with substantial water volume. Sauropods will wade into deep water — this is normal behaviour.

### Food
- Herbivores: place ground feeders (nut, fruit, leaf, grass) appropriate to the species. Place more than you think you need — feeders deplete over time. Multiple feeders reduce stress from dominant animals monopolising a single source.
- Carnivores: live prey feeders or carnivore feeders depending on size. Some carnivore species list Prey as an Environmental Need — these require a live prey source and will hunt.
- In cohabitation enclosures, always provide multiple feeders. Dominant animals will drive others away from a single feeder, causing hunger stress.

### Foliage
Trees and plants fulfil forest and cover requirements. Pre-populate the enclosure with foliage before releasing dinosaurs — comfort updates for forest coverage as the dinosaur moves through its Territory. Some species also require nests to be out of view of guests to maintain comfort; dense foliage around nesting areas solves this.

## Nests and Juveniles
Nests are a new enclosure requirement in JWE3. Place nests appropriate to the species type (ground, avian, or aquatic) within the dinosaur's Territory. Nests enable both natural breeding and structured Breeding Programmes.

When juveniles are present, expect additional management demands:
- Juvenile needs may differ from adult needs — scan them separately
- Territory space requirements increase as juveniles grow into adults
- Parents will aggressively defend young against perceived threats, including other enclosure residents
- Juveniles are small enough to explore further than adults expect and may interact with species their parents would ignore; only cohabit juveniles with Liked or safe Neutral species
- Reinforce fence coverage and ensure Maintenance and Ranger Posts have good enclosure overlap

## Fence Types and Security
Fences have security ratings from 2 to 5. If a dinosaur's security rating exceeds the fence's, it can break out. Electrified fences raise the effective security rating; unpowered electric fences drop to a lower base rating and should be treated as unelectrified.

- Small theropods (Velociraptor, Dilophosaurus) will climb non-electrified fences
- Large theropods will headbutt fences and gates
- Thyreophorans (Stegosaurus, Ankylosaur) use tail attacks on fences
- Unhappy dinosaurs attack fences continuously
- Intelligent-trait dinosaurs will probe fences whenever they spot food, prey, or guests nearby — keep sight lines clear and use maximum-security fencing

When replacing a fence panel it briefly disappears — dinosaurs can and will exploit this window. Replace panels during calm periods and have a Capture Team on standby.

## Cohabitation
Check the Territory tab of any dinosaur to see its Liked, Neutral, and Disliked species.

- **Liked species** cohabit peacefully and generally never fight; exceptions include Compies being eaten by small or medium carnivores (this is intended behaviour)
- **Neutral species** raise cohabitation stress on each other; too many together triggers a Cohabitation Issue
- **Disliked species** fight to the death — never mix these

Most herbivores can tolerate one Neutral species before hitting stress thresholds. The Tolerant trait effectively adds one additional Neutral cohab. Cross-Species Adaptation (from Genetic Modification) also improves cohabitation tolerance.

Enclosure size matters for cohabitation. In a small enclosure, overlapping Territories cause greater stress than in a large one where species can naturally zone themselves apart.

## Population and Social Needs
Each species has minimum and maximum social counts (same species) and a total population count. Exceeding the maximum triggers overcrowding stress. Falling below the minimum stresses social species. Some species also have a specific requirement for the number of males present in the Territory — check the Social Needs tab.

Release enough of a species to meet social minimums before expecting stable comfort. When breeding raises population, monitor whether the enclosure is approaching overcrowding thresholds.

## Ranger and Maintenance Coverage
Place Ranger Posts inside or adjacent to each enclosure so that the post radius covers all dinosaurs. Ranger Teams perform welfare scans, restock feeders, and check on juveniles. MVU Teams diagnose and treat illnesses and injuries. Capture Teams tranquilise dinosaurs for transport or treatment.

Ensure coverage overlaps slightly between posts — animals near the edge of a radius may be missed. Assign all three team types (Ranger, MVU, Capture) to each Ranger Post in active enclosures.

Maintenance Posts should cover all fence panels, generators, and facilities. Unpowered facilities decay quickly during storms; solid Maintenance coverage is the best prevention.

## Enclosure Types
- **Land enclosures**: fenced areas for terrestrial species
- **Aviaries**: modular domed structures for pterosaurs; require an internal Aviary Hatchery; can be linked together for larger flying spaces
- **Lagoons**: for marine reptiles; require large water areas and a Lagoon Hatchery

## Escape Prevention Checklist
- Fence security rating ≥ dinosaur security rating, and fences are electrified and powered
- All comfort needs are in the white (no red bars)
- Population is within the social min/max range
- No Disliked species sharing the enclosure
- Fence coverage is complete with no gaps; Maintenance Teams are assigned
- Ranger Posts cover the full enclosure area
- Intelligent-trait animals have no sight lines to guests or feeders outside the enclosure
